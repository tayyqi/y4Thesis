Investigating the Effects of Digital Media Consumption on
Bedtime Procrastination for Young Adults in Singapore

Tay Ying Qi | A0190374A

Abstract

In Singapore, it is inevitable to be exposed to digital media, with Singaporeans spending an average of 8 hours and 7 minutes online daily, significantly greater than the global average of 6 hours and 4 minutes (Neo, 2021). In recent years, bedtime procrastination has been in the spotlight as it leads to sleep deprivation which reduces overall wellbeing and productivity (Hafner et al., 2017). This dissertation studies the effects of digital media on bedtime procrastination in young adults and proposes design concepts that could intervene and reduce this behaviour.

Introduction

Bedtime procrastination is the action of going to bed later than intended, without external reasons to do so while being fully aware of its negative consequences (Kroese et al., 2014). This behaviour is closely associated with brain-stimulating activities such as watching TV or using smartphones before sleep. With the onset of the pandemic, the shift to remote work has blurred the boundaries between work and rest, increasing the propensity for late bedtimes (Schlundt, 2021).

In Singapore, digital media usage plays the biggest role in this behaviour (Chee & Massar, 2021). In the 2021 global sleep study by Philips, 69% of Singapore’s respondents who use their phones before falling asleep admit that it leads them to fall asleep later than they would like to, with a majority rolling through social media or watching videos in the time before sleep. This is in line with the Singapore-based study conducted by Yong Loo Lin School of Medicine, reporting that 65% of respondents listed engagement with some type of electronic media to be one of the activities that keep them from going to bed (Chee & Massar, 2021).

Bedtime procrastination leads to sleep deprivation, resulting in a loss in productivity (Furuichi et al., 2020), dip in mental health, and overall wellbeing (Hafner et al., 2017). This is detrimental in Singapore’s productivity-driven society, therefore it is essential to take steps to reduce any unnecessary pushing back of bedtimes.

Studies have shown that bedtime procrastination may relate to general procrastination (Kroese et al., 2014). In addition, general procrastination is the most common in early adulthood (McCown & Roberts, 1994). In this dissertation, I will be investigating the effects of digital media consumption on bedtime procrastination of young adults in their 20s in Singapore, aiming to identify design opportunities to intervene in their bedtime procrastination behaviours.

Digital Media and Bedtime Procrastination

The pandemic has brought about changes in lifestyle patterns of university students, increasing bedtime procrastination through digital media use (Cellini et al., 2020). Singapore has a high mobile penetration rate of 157.7% as of June 2021 (Infocomm Media Development Authority, 2021). The increasingly portable and accessible technologies have made it inevitable to be exposed to attention-grabbing content available on the internet from a young age (Tang, 2019). This may be why young adults, who are mostly Generation Z, are heavily reliant on technology and more prone to phone addiction. The portability of a phone also makes it much more convenient to be propped up next to the pillow to scroll through while lying in bed.

Tristan Harris, a founder of Time Well Spent and ex-design ethicist at Google, highlighted 3 features that make phones addictive (Haubursin, 2018):

  1. Unpredictable content of notifications
    These exist as push notifications that hide and obscure information, enticing the user to click on it to read the full contents.
  2. Attention-grabbing colours
    Application icons use bright warm colours such as red and orange to draw attention.
  3. Infinite scrolling and video autoplay
    By introducing a never-ending flow of content, users are less likely to break away from their phones as there is no visual indication of an endpoint.

Of all applications, the stimulating and rewarding nature of social media seems to be the most addictive (Levenson, 2016). As social media involves interactive screen time, which is more engaging as compared to passive activities like watching television (Hale & Guan, 2015), it is harder to fall asleep.

Bedtime Procrastination
Bedtime procrastination occurs at the end of the day, where there may be fewer resources available for self-regulation due to fatigue (Kamphorst et al., 2018). As compared to daytime phone usage, it may be harder for users to disconnect from the addictive content constantly being fed to them as the desire to watch one more episode is far greater than the desire to be awake for an important meeting the next day. Moreover, users may lose track of time due to the lack of visual indication of the time passed while being absorbed to their screens, making it harder to break away from their screens. As users fall into the bottomless vortex of digital media, their sleep time is delayed which leads to sleep deprivation.

Wind-Down Time

Wind-Down time refers to the window before sleep when your body shifts from an “on” to “off” state, usually occurring one to two hours before sleep (Rise Science, n.d.). Sleep experts recommend engaging in passive tasks to help the body and mind slow down to a relaxed state, making it easier to fall asleep and ensuring good sleep quality.

In a self-conducted survey, with 37 respondents aged 18-25 years old, 86.5% reported using their phones right before sleep. Additionally, more than half of respondents spent 30 minutes or less preparing for sleep, with 21.6% not preparing for sleep at all (Figure 2).

Figure 2. Responses from a self-conducted survey in September 2021.

“What else am I supposed to do?”

When asked about the reasons for phone usage before bed, respondents found a need to unwind using their phones, feeling the need to connect through social media and relax through YouTube videos. We can derive that there is a strong dependency on digital media to unwind at the end of the day despite research showing the adverse effects of phone usage before sleep.

Identifying design space and design explorations

As discussed above, bedtime procrastination in young adults largely occurs on mobile phones, due to the addictive nature of digital media. Thus, I will be focusing on the phone interface and wind-down time as my design space. In the following section, I will describe early design explorations that revolve around this design space.

Design exploration 1: Customising sleep reminders based on schedule
1.1 Types of reminders

The first exploration focuses on customised sleep reminders based on current activities on the phone and future schedules. Previous designs for sleep reminders relied on a pre-set sleep time but did not account for pre-sleep phone usage or daily schedules (e.g. Samsung Bedtime, Oura).

Figure 1.1. Samsung bedtime sends notifications based on the sleep time that is input by the user. These timings cannot be varied for each day.

Figure 1.2. Oura uses sleep tracking to determine a fixed bedtime every day. Sleep notifications were sent 2 hours before sleep time, which may be too long to prepare for sleep.

Across a period of 4 days, sleep reminders were sent to 4 participants, 3 females aged 22 and 1 male aged 24, at 4 distinct intervals. Before the experiment, participants had sent their desired sleep and wake times. There were 4 messages sent: 2 nudges to prepare for sleep, a notification of sleep time, and a check-in message at wake time.

Figure 2. Examples of messages sent to participants

On days 1 to 2, the messages addressed participants in first person as a more direct approach. On days 3 to 4, messages were written about the participants’ “ideal self”, which sets up a role model for the participant. Additionally, there was slight customisation to participant’s pre-sleep habits in the second nudge to prepare for sleep.

User feedback

Out of 4 participants, 3 felt that the messages which addressed participants directly were more effective at evoking guilt at not following their sleep times, while those written in third person felt quite disconnected. Generally, the messages were useful in increasing awareness of one’s sleep schedule and their need for sleep. However, these messages were not particularly effective in pushing participants to stop using their phones and sleep.

User interviews with 4 other university students found that sleep times were largely dependent on the time they had to wake up for the next day. Lessons in university did not start at the same time every day (Figure 3), which resulted in a drastically varying sleep schedule throughout the week, and thus less motivation to follow the reminders which were sent at fixed times every day.

Figure 3. Different timetables of university students

1.2 Customising notifications to important events through calendar synchronization

To motivate users to follow the sleep reminders, the reminders could be customised according to specific important events from the user’s calendar. This is because scheduled calendar events are likely to be important or special, such as appointments, all-day events, and to-do items (Tungare et al., 2008). This helps to set a short-term goal, framing it such that sticking to their sleep schedule would help achieve this goal.

3 distinct examples of such notifications were made and shown to 6 university students for user feedback.

Figure 4. Prototypes of notifications catered to specific events in the user’s calendar

User feedback

Users were more likely to prepare to sleep when presented with notifications that were relevant to them. As most feedback was from university students, notifications relating to assignments were slightly effective. The notification relating to skincare received surprisingly positive reactions, with a respondent stating that based on experience, sleeping early does improve the appearance of the skin, and would sleep on time.

Design exploration 2: Disruptive User Interface

People tend to follow visual cues rather than internal reminders (Haubursin, 2018). As bedtime procrastination mostly happens on the phone, these cues should be present on the phone interface to effectively break the flow of media browsing. Additionally, visual cues serve as a step up from passive reminders by directly inhibiting phone usage, which would be useful for those who are less sensitive to reminders.

I have explored various design possibilities of disruptive UI within a spectrum of subtle to obvious. In the first phase of user testing, 6 university students were presented with a mobile device with an interactive interface showing the effects of grayscale, scroll lag and screen blur. In the second phase, 6 university students were sent videos of social media interfaces mimicking pre-sleep browsing behaviour with a gradually decreasing screen size.

Figure 5. A spectrum of proposed disruptive UI designs from subtle to obvious

2.1 Grayscale

Figure 6. Grayscale on Samsung

When the designated time frame approaches, the phone interface turns to grayscale over 2 seconds. This reduces the distraction from colourful icons, pictures, and videos, reducing the engagement level with the user. This feature is existing as part of in-built digital wellbeing applications on various smartphones.

Even though it was effective in reducing engagement for applications such as Instagram and YouTube, users expressed that they would hop over to text-based applications, such as Reddit, which already do not have much media content. Therefore, I decided to explore disruptions that reduced the readability of the phone interface.



2.2 Scroll Lag

Figure 7. Screen recording of scroll lag prototype

Users interacted with a social media interface with an erratic scroll lag, evoking frustration and making users put down their phones.

2.3 Screen Blur

Figure 8. Screen recording of screen blur prototype

As users scrolled through a social media page, the screen became increasingly blurry, making images and text undecipherable.

User feedback

Scroll lag and screen blur are subtle interface disruptions reminiscent of technical difficulties. Users reported feeling frustrated, angry and felt more awake as there was the need to fix the technical difficulty at hand, with the first reaction to restart the application, followed by restarting the phone itself. With that, I moved to a more obvious interface disruption through decreasing the size of the screen gradually over 7.5 minutes.

2.4 Decreasing screen size

Users were sent a different video of a shrinking screen near their sleep times, over 3 days: a top-down shutter, a shrinking rectangle and a frame loading bar. Each video consisted of different content to mimic the unpredictability of recommended content in social media.

Figure 9. From left to right: Top-down shutter, shrinking rectangle and frame loading bar concepts

2.4.1 Top-down shutter

Figure 10. Prototype of top-down shutter

This was most effective in stopping phone usage as it cut off important parts in the video, which evoked feelings of annoyance. One participant noted that she felt more tired as she had to use more energy to look at her screen.

2.4.2 Shrinking rectangle

Figure 11. Prototype of shrinking rectangle

As most of the content was visible in the centre of the screen, this interface was not as effective in deterring users from using their phones but did not annoy users as much. Some users highlighted that they were actively noticing the reduction in screen size, which may have contributed to the shorter time taken to notice the applied effect.

2.4.3 Frame loading bar

Figure 12. Prototype of frame loading bar

The faster speed of the moving bar was much more noticeable compared to the previous 2 explorations. Users highlighted that their attention was drawn more to the moving bar than the content, which gave them a sense of urgency of the time passing, making it the most effective in depicting the approaching sleep time. On the other hand, the distractive nature of the animation led some users to be fascinated and not feel like sleeping.

In general, users stopped watching the videos when too much content was blocked off due to a loss of interest. Negative feelings of annoyance are effective in deterring users from using their phones. On the other hand, hiding content may lead to curiosity and being more awake. A balance must be found to evoke a sense of urgency without evoking negative emotions or fascination with the applied animations.

Conclusion and future steps

Having analysed the effect of digital media on bedtime procrastination, I will be using customised sleep notifications and obvious disruptive interfaces as key nudges in developing an appropriate intervention to reduce bedtime procrastination in young adults that unnecessarily push back their sleep times. Further explorations are needed to ensure that the disruptive UI is obvious without invoking negative emotions.
